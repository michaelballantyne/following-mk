# rember-2: the follower changes the answer

The first data point where the follower *demonstrably* helps — not via
speed, not via refutation pruning, but by steering the search to a
different (and better) answer.

## Setup

`synthesis/rember-2.scm` removes one expression from the rember body,
replacing the whole recursive-call expression `(rember e d)` with a
fresh hole `,p`.  Five examples, in the "good" order:

1. `(rember 5 '())` → `'()`           — base case
2. `(rember 6 (cons 6 '()))` → `'()`  — then-branch on a length-1 list
3. `(rember 7 (cons 3 '()))` → `'(3)` — else-branch on length-1
4. `(rember 4 (cons 3 (cons 6 (cons 4 '()))))` → `'(3 6)` — length-3 removal at pos 2
5. `(rember 4 (cons 3 (cons 6 (cons 4 (cons 7 '())))))` → `'(3 6 7)` — length-4 removal at pos 2

Ex5 is the key one: it's longer than every trick answer the baseline
finds for ex1–4.

## Results

| version | answer found | real time |
|---|---|---|
| no follower | `(match d ['() d] [(cons _.0 _.1) (cons _.0 (match _.1 ['() _.2] [(cons _.3 _.4) _.4]))])` | 19 ms |
| with follower | `(rember e d)` | 225 ms |

The baseline's answer is a *nested-match trick*: it walks `d` directly
via two levels of `match` and picks out the element at position 1 of
`d`.  For lists up to length 4 with removal at position 2 (i.e. position
1 of `d`), this happens to give the right answer.  It's the first valid
shape the main search's conde ordering reaches.

The follower's answer is the actual recursive call.  It's a later shape
in the conde order, and the main search reaches the nested-match trick
first when run alone.  But inside a follower, `evalo/d` runs all five
examples forward on concrete inputs, and the incremental
determinacy-mode checks refute enough of the intermediate hypotheses
leading to the nested-match shape that `(rember e d)` becomes the first
answer surviving all five examples.

## Counters tell the same story

With-follower run:

```
*==-counter*: 7233
*==/d-counter*: 187328
*fail-counter*: 490
*singleton-succeed-counter*: 1
*non-singleton-succeed-counter*: 1658
*externally-productive-trigger-counter*: 16
*externally-unproductive-trigger-counter*: 1643
```

This is the first benchmark where `externally-productive-trigger-counter`
is non-zero.  16 triggers committed new bindings to the outer-visible
`p` variable — i.e. the follower *propagated* 16 times.  Combined with
490 `fail` pruning events, the follower is actively shaping what the
main search sees, not just watching it.

The overhead is very real: 187K `==/d` vs the baseline's 16K `==`, and
the follower version is ~12× slower in wall-clock.  But it finds the
right answer where the baseline finds a coincidental lookalike.

## What this means

Until rember-2, every benchmark we'd looked at either:

- terminated quickly with the baseline alone (so the follower was pure
  overhead, e.g. rember-1), or
- didn't terminate at all with or without the follower (so the
  follower couldn't demonstrate any value, e.g. most of
  `synthesis/rember-full.scm`).

rember-2 is in a different regime: the baseline terminates quickly but
finds a *trick* answer, and the follower's propagation steers the
search to the intended recursive answer.  That is a real and
demonstrable effect from the determinacy-directed propagation
mechanism — independent of its overhead cost.

The overhead question is orthogonal: the same steering effect *could*
be achieved more cheaply if we found a way to avoid re-running the
follower goal from scratch on every trigger.  But first, this confirms
the mechanism is doing something the baseline can't, on at least one
benchmark.

## Open questions

- Does the follower find the recursive answer on the full rember
  synthesis in `synthesis/rember-full.scm`, or does the `--main-unsound-depth`
  /`--check-follower-every` knobs turn those timeouts into successes?
- What's the smallest hole that still exhibits the steering effect?
  (rember-1 is too small; the baseline trivially finds the one-symbol
  answer.)
- Does the follower still pick `(rember e d)` over the trick with
  `--check-follower-every 10` or `20`?  That would show the steering
  effect doesn't require firing on every conde.

;; one symbol, the hole replaces the entire recursive call
;; `(rember e d)` in the else-branch, so the synthesizer has to find
;; a 3-element application, not a single symbol.  Expected answer:
;; p = (rember e d).
;;
;; Five examples in the "good" order.  Ex1 and ex2 hit the base cases;
;; ex3 hits the else-branch once; ex4 removes at position 2 of a 3-elem
;; list; ex5 removes at position 2 of a 4-elem list, leaving a 3-elem
